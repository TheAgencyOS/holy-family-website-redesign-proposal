Section F

Post-Launch Support

We build for you to own. Post-launch support ensures a smooth transition, not ongoing dependency. Every deliverable is designed so Rick Mitchell's team can run this site confidently and independently after the engagement ends.

F.1

90-day post-launch support

A 90-day stabilization period is included in the base project cost at no additional charge. The clock starts the day the production site goes live on Pantheon and covers the window between launch and the point where your team has full operational confidence in the new platform.

Included

Bug fixes and defect resolution

Unlimited during the 90-day window. Any defect traceable to our deliverables (code, templates, blocks, forms, integrations) is diagnosed and resolved at no additional cost. We do not cap hours for fixing our own work.

Included

Content updates

Up to 20 hours of editorial support: page edits, image swaps, copy revisions, and new pages built from existing block patterns. Hours are tracked transparently and reported weekly.

Included

Form testing and CRM verification

Gravity Forms to Slate webhook monitoring for the first 30 days post-launch. Every submission is verified end-to-end. Slate Ping validation confirms behavioral tracking fires on each page template.

Included

Analytics setup and verification

GA4 event validation across every conversion point, GTM container audit, and delivery of a baseline GA4 dashboard tied to the enrollment metrics defined during discovery.

Included

Training reinforcement

Two 1-hour virtual follow-up Q and A sessions, scheduled at your team's request. These are grounded in real questions from daily editorial work, not hypotheticals.

Response time commitments

Severity Definition Response Resolution target

What is not included in the 90-day window

Major feature additions outside the original scope. Third-party vendor support (Slate, Gravity Forms, Crazy Egg, Hotjar). Server and infrastructure administration beyond the WordPress application layer (Pantheon platform, DNS, SSL, CDN). Content creation: new copy, new graphics, photography, or video. We help diagnose whether an issue sits on our side or a vendor's, but we do not operate as a vendor support desk.

F.2

Training and knowledge transfer

Training is a structured curriculum, not an afterthought bolted to the end of the project. Every session uses Holy Family University's actual content in your actual WordPress environment. No generic demos, no sandbox site with fake programs. When your team walks out of a session, they have already done the work they will be doing on Monday morning.

Administrator training Two half-day sessions, 3 hours each

Audience: content editors, department web liaisons, and marketing team members who create or update content in the WordPress dashboard.

Session 1: WordPress foundations and content editing
  • WordPress dashboard orientation and menu structure
  • Block Editor and Gutenberg, including every custom block and block pattern built for this project
  • Content types: creating, editing, and publishing Programs, Faculty, Events, and News with the correct fields
  • Media library: upload, organize, crop, and add alt text; file size and format requirements
  • Content workflow: draft, review, schedule, publish, revision history, and revert
Session 2: Governance, SEO, and accessibility
  • User management and role assignment (Administrator, Editor, Author, Contributor)
  • SEO basics with Rank Math: meta titles, descriptions, Open Graph, program page optimization
  • Accessibility in the editor: heading hierarchy, meaningful link text, required alt text, accessible tables
  • Content governance: the lifecycle policy established during the project, ownership, archival, update cadence
  • 301 redirects: creating and managing them when URLs change
Developer training Two full-day sessions, 6 hours each

Audience: Rick Mitchell and any technical staff who will maintain the codebase, deploy updates, and extend the site after handoff.

Session 1: Architecture, theme, and plugin deep dive
  • Theme architecture: template hierarchy, template parts, block theme patterns, file layout
  • Child theme customization and the upgrade path
  • Custom plugin walkthrough: every plugin built for this project, what it does, how it is structured
  • ACF field groups: adding fields, modifying groups, relationships with templates
  • Block patterns and variations: creating, modifying, registering custom block styles
  • Coding standards: WordPress Coding Standards, ESLint config, PHP_CodeSniffer rules
Session 2: DevOps, deployment, and extending the site
  • Pantheon workflow: Dev, Test, and Live environments, Multidev, commit to deploy
  • Git workflow: branching, commits, merges, and branch protection rules
  • WP-CLI and Terminus: cache clear, search-replace, plugin updates, database exports
  • Deployment checklist: staging verification, visual regression, go or no-go
  • Autopilot: automated core and plugin updates with visual regression, and when to intervene manually
  • Extending the site end-to-end: adding a new custom post type, a new page template, or a new Gravity Form wired to Slate

Post-training office hours

For 30 calendar days after the final training session, your team has access to weekly 30-minute virtual office hours for question triage. Open agenda, first-come first-served, bring whatever came up during the week. Office hours are separate from the 90-day support window: they exist for "how do I do X" questions, not bug reports.

F.4

Post-launch optimization roadmap

Independent of any retainer engagement, we recommend the following milestones for the first year after launch. These can be executed by Rick Mitchell's team, by [TODO: Agency Name] on retainer, or by any qualified partner.

  1. 1

    Launch plus 30 days: conversion baseline report

    Establish post-launch metrics for RFI submissions, application starts, bounce rate, and pages per session. This becomes the yardstick for everything that follows.

  2. 2

    Launch plus 60 days: heatmap and session recording review

    Review Crazy Egg and Hotjar data to identify UX friction on program pages and the admissions flow. Flag 3 to 5 concrete fixes.

  3. 3

    Launch plus 90 days: SEO health check and redirect audit

    Verify every 301 redirect resolves correctly. Surface crawl errors, indexing issues, and schema problems in Google Search Console.

  4. 4

    Launch plus 6 months: content freshness audit

    Flag pages that have not been updated since launch, review the news archival policy, and assess whether content governance is holding up in practice.

  5. 5

    Launch plus 6 months: first A/B test cycle

    Test one high-impact hypothesis: program page CTA placement, RFI form length, or homepage hero messaging. Ship the winner.

  6. 6

    Launch plus 12 months: annual performance review

    Compare year one post-launch metrics against the pre-launch baseline. Deliver a brief report with year two recommendations.

This roadmap is included as a project deliverable. Your team can execute it independently, with our retainer, or with any qualified partner.
