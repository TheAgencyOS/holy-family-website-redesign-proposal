Section B

How we will build it

This is a content strategy engagement wearing a website redesign's clothes. The 1,601 URLs do not need a new visual layer. They need to be audited, consolidated, re-architected around enrollment, rebuilt on WordPress, and handed back to Rick Mitchell's team in a form they can run for the next five years without calling us.

B.1

Project approach

Three principles organize every decision on this engagement, and they resolve the tradeoffs that will come up in week four and week twenty the same way. Rick Mitchell, Director of Digital Experience Design, writes code himself and maintains Holy Family University's internal design system. Katherine Primus, VP for Marketing and Communications, owns the budget and the enrollment targets it defends. The principles below are the ones we will reach for when their two vantage points pull in different directions.

Enrollment is the organizing metric. Not the only metric, but the tiebreaker. When two good options compete, we pick the one that makes it easier for a prospective student to find the right program, understand its value, and take the next step. Program discovery, information requests, and application starts get the most rigorous design attention, the fastest load times, and the clearest calls to action. Current students, alumni, faculty, and donors are served through defined pathways that do not compete with enrollment conversion.

Content strategy precedes visual design. The current site has 1,601 indexed URLs across 180 plus distinct top-level sections. That is evidence of organic growth without centralized governance, and a new visual layer applied to that structure would produce a better-looking version of the same navigational complexity. The audit drives the information architecture. The information architecture drives the wireframes. The wireframes drive the visual design. No reordering.

The site we build is the site your team runs. Rick Mitchell's team will own this website for the next five or more years. Every component, every content model, every workflow is designed for maintainability by a lean internal team, not for ongoing agency dependency. Documented component library, content templates with guardrails, structured knowledge transfer. When we leave, your team should be faster and more capable than it was before we arrived.

B.2

Discovery and strategy methodology

University websites fail when departments feel unheard and succeed when departments feel represented inside a coherent structure. Discovery is six weeks of structured listening, synthesis, and research before a single wireframe is drawn, run in three phases with a documented handoff at the end of each one.

1

Institutional listening (weeks 1 to 2)

Structured interviews and workshops with executive sponsors, the academic deans of the four schools (Arts and Sciences, Business and Technology, Education, Nursing and Health Sciences), enrollment and admissions, student services, Rick Mitchell's digital and IT team, and student representatives. Each session runs 60 to 90 minutes with a pre-session questionnaire and a written summary delivered within 48 hours for validation. The goal is an auditable record of stakeholder input that prevents scope disputes later.

2

Synthesis and prioritization (week 3)

A scored Stakeholder Findings Report maps every surfaced need against three criteria: enrollment impact, implementation feasibility, and audience reach. Needs that score high on all three become core requirements. Needs that score high on impact but low on feasibility enter the Phase 2 backlog. Needs that score low on impact are documented and deferred. Every stakeholder sees why their request landed where it did.

3

Content strategy and research (weeks 4 to 6)

Analytics deep dive on the existing GA4, Crazy Egg, and Hotjar stack. Competitive analysis against three named peers. Task-based user research with real prospective and current students. The full 1,601-URL content audit begins and runs in parallel through visual design, because the audit is the single largest workstream in the project and it cannot wait.

  • Task-based usability testing. 8 to 10 participants across three audience segments (prospective undergraduate, prospective graduate, current student) attempt core tasks on the current site. We are testing findability and navigation paths, not visual preferences.
  • Search query analysis. Internal site search logs reveal what users cannot find through navigation. The top 100 queries are categorized by intent and mapped to an IA or content fix.
  • Competitive user testing. Three named peer institutions are tested with the same task protocol to establish what good looks like in the minds of the university's prospective students: La Salle University (direct peer, has a hero program finder), Chestnut Hill College (scale peer, similar Catholic positioning), and Creighton University (the modern Catholic university benchmark in our sample).
  • AI search visibility audit. How the university and its peers appear today in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews for queries like "nursing programs in Philadelphia" and "Catholic universities near me."
B.3

Information architecture approach

The current site has more than 180 distinct top-level URL sections. That is not navigation, it is a filing cabinet with no drawers. The fact that /nursing silently redirects to /grad-nursing is the kind of IA debt that accumulates when departments can create top-level URLs without architectural review: a program accidentally renames itself at the URL level, the old URL is kept as a redirect, and ten years later nobody remembers why. Our first job is to rationalize that structure into eight to twelve primary sections that a prospective student can scan and a content editor can maintain.

Proposed primary information architecture with eight to twelve top-level sections
Section path Primary audience Content
/programs/ Prospective students All academic programs with structured data, organized by school, level, and format
/admissions/ Prospective students Application process, financial aid, campus visits, a single smart RFI form
/student-life/ Prospective and current students Housing, activities, campus ministry, support resources
/about/ All audiences Leadership, mission, history, accreditation, Sisters of the Holy Family of Nazareth
/news/ All audiences Current news and the Bold Ideas Expert Hub, two-year rolling archive
/athletics/ Students and community Sports programs, schedules, rosters, recruitment
/alumni/ Alumni and donors Engagement, giving, reunions, chapter activity
/resources/ Current students and faculty IT services, library, career services, registrar, policies
/directory/ All audiences Faculty and staff profiles with department and school filtering
Rather than building separate prospective-student and current-student portals that create content duplication, we layer audience-specific entry pages over a shared content structure. The content is the same; the entry experience is personalized.

A prospective undergraduate arriving from a paid search campaign sees a hero focused on program discovery and campus visit scheduling. A current student arriving from a bookmarked link sees quick access to registration, the library, and student services. The underlying /programs/ and /resources/ content is the same in both cases, and there is only one copy of it for an editor to maintain.

  • Card sorting (open and closed). 15 to 20 participants across audience segments test whether our proposed groupings match user mental models before anything is locked in.
  • Tree testing. Task success rates on the proposed navigation structure are measured before a pixel of visual design is applied.
  • First-click testing. On key tasks (find the Nursing program requirements, apply for financial aid, request information about the MBA) we validate that the primary navigation labels actually work.
  • Comparison testing. The proposed IA is benchmarked against the current IA so improvement is measurable, not asserted.
B.4

Design process

Design begins with evidence, not mood boards. By the time a designer opens Figma, we have stakeholder interviews, usability findings on the current site, analytics behavior, and a validated IA in hand. The process then moves through four phases, each one gated by a tangible artifact the Steering Committee signs off on.

1

Low-fidelity wireframes

Grayscale layouts for 8 to 10 key page templates focused on content hierarchy, component placement, and conversion flow. No color, no imagery. This is where we validate structure without aesthetic distraction.

2

High-fidelity mockups

Full visual design applied to approved wireframes. Two design directions for the homepage and one direction for interior pages, all built from the same component system. Typography, color, photography, and micro-interactions are all resolved here.

3

Interactive prototypes

Clickable prototypes for the three most critical user journeys: program discovery to RFI submission, admissions information to application start, and homepage to campus visit request. These prototypes are tested with 5 to 8 users before any production code is written.

4

Design QA

Our designers review every implemented component against approved mockups throughout development, flagging deviations before they compound. Design QA is not a phase at the end. It is a continuous checkpoint that runs alongside every sprint.

Respecting Rick Mitchell's design system. Rick has already invested in an internal design system. We will not discard that work. Our sequence is audit, translate, extend, then document, in that order. We audit what exists, translate the tokens and patterns that carry over into the new WordPress block theme, extend the library to cover the components the new site needs, and document everything in a Figma file that mirrors the production code. We do not replace a design system that is already working.

The mobile-first discipline is non-negotiable. Over 60 percent of prospective student traffic arrives on a phone. We design for the small screen first and enhance for larger viewports, rather than designing for desktop and then compressing. Touch targets meet the WCAG 2.2 minimum. Form fields use the correct input types for mobile keyboards. Critical conversion paths (Request Info, Apply, Visit) are reachable within one thumb tap from any page. If a content block does not work on a phone screen, it does not ship.

B.5

Development methodology

We build on WordPress using modern practices that produce maintainable, performant, and secure code, and every technical standard is enforced in the CI pipeline so nothing ships that silently bypasses the rules.

  • WordPress Coding Standards enforced via PHPCS with the WordPress-Extra ruleset on every commit.
  • Semantic HTML5 as the structural foundation. Proper heading hierarchy, landmark regions, native form elements, and ARIA attributes only where native semantics are insufficient.
  • Utility-first CSS compiled from design tokens. No specificity wars, no !important overrides, and a single source of truth for color, type, spacing, and motion values.
  • Progressive enhancement JavaScript. Core functionality works without JS. Enhanced interactions (mega-menu, form validation, lazy loading, scroll spy) layer on top.
  • PHP 8.2 plus with strict typing where applicable, following WordPress Plugin API conventions for all custom functionality.
  • Git-based workflow on Pantheon. Feature branches, pull requests with mandatory code review, automated testing on merge, promotion through Dev, Test, and Live environments.

The Lighthouse baseline we measured on the current homepage on April 10, 2026 is the floor we are building away from. Desktop LCP is 11.42 seconds. Mobile LCP is 21.19 seconds. Mobile Performance is 51. Those are not numbers a nursing applicant will wait out on a phone between clinicals. Our performance budgets, enforced in CI, are below.

  • Automated axe-core in CI. Every pull request is scanned for WCAG rule violations before it can merge.
  • Visual regression testing. Percy or BackstopJS compares screenshots across browsers and viewports on every pull request.
  • Lighthouse CI with thresholds. Performance, Accessibility, and Best Practices scores are enforced. A regression blocks the merge.
  • PHPCS linting. WordPress-Extra ruleset catches style and security issues at commit time.
  • Link checking. Automated crawl on staging catches 404s before promotion.
  • Manual cross-browser testing. Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge on current and current-minus-one versions.
  • Manual screen reader testing. VoiceOver on macOS and iOS, NVDA on Windows, on every interactive component and form flow.
B.6

Content strategy and migration

This is the heart of the proposal. Content is architecture. Migrating 1,601 URLs from Drupal to WordPress is a content strategy engagement, not a data transfer exercise, and if it is treated as a data transfer we will have rebuilt the same navigational mess in a new CMS. The audit decides what moves and what does not. The content model decides what shape the surviving content takes. The migration pipeline is how we execute those decisions at scale without losing SEO equity.

1

Audit and consolidation decisions

Every URL cataloged with page type, last modified date, traffic volume, inbound link count, and owner. Each page enters the classification framework above. The output is a signed-off inventory of what moves, what merges, what archives, and what is deleted.

2

Content model build

WordPress Custom Post Types, ACF field groups, and taxonomies for Programs, Faculty, Events, News, and Policies. Required fields, optional fields, and editorial guidelines documented in the editor experience itself so consistency survives staff turnover.

3

Bulk automated migration

FG Drupal to WordPress Premium reads the Drupal 10 database directly and transfers content nodes, taxonomies, media, and URL structures into WordPress. WP All Import handles refinement passes that map migrated content into the right Custom Post Types with proper ACF field assignments.

4

AI-assisted cleanup pass

Batch LLM processing normalizes legacy HTML, generates missing meta descriptions, writes alt text for untagged images, and flags content that needs human rewriting. This is not AI generating content for the university. It is AI accelerating the mechanical cleanup so human writers focus on the strategic work.

5

Manual quality review

Human editors verify every migrated program page, every admissions page, and the top 50 traffic pages against the source. Spot checks across the remaining migrated inventory. Nothing ships to production without a human sign-off on enrollment-critical content.

6

Redirect map validation

Every one of the 1,601 current URLs is tested against its new URL, programmatically via automated crawl and manually for the top 100 traffic pages and every externally linked URL. The validated redirect map is a project deliverable, not a Word document in a shared drive.

Our AI framework is simple. AI drafts, humans decide, code executes, tests verify. No content ships without human review. AI is used to accelerate mechanical work (HTML normalization, metadata generation, alt text drafts, similarity scoring for redirects) so our writers and Rick's team can spend their time on the decisions that actually matter.

SEO, AEO, and GEO

Traditional SEO is necessary but no longer sufficient. Prospective students are increasingly using ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews to research programs, and the content AI surfaces from the university's site is often the first impression a student receives. Our optimization plan covers all three layers.

  • Schema markup plan. EducationalOrganization on the homepage and About pages. EducationalOccupationalProgram on every program page with name, provider, cost, duration, occupational category, and salary signals. FAQPage on admissions, financial aid, and student life pages (FAQ schema has the highest citation probability in AI-generated answers). Event for open houses and recruitment events. Article on news content.
  • llms.txt at the domain root. We verified on April 10 that holyfamily.edu/llms.txt returns 404. We will place a machine-readable Markdown file at the root listing the university's key institutional pages: program directories, admissions, tuition and financial aid facts, application deadlines. This is a low-effort, high-signal AEO quick win.
  • Content architecture for AI citation. Grade 6 to 8 reading level on public-facing pages, specific statistics rather than vague claims, FAQ sections on pages that answer a clear question, and a regular freshness cadence on enrollment-critical content. AI engines preferentially cite recent, specific, readable content.
  • Semantic redirect mapping via sentence-transformers and FAISS embeddings. Every old URL is embedded and compared to every new URL. Matches above 0.98 similarity auto-accept. Matches below 0.98 enter a manual review queue. This scales the redirect work from weeks of manual mapping to days of targeted human judgment on the edge cases.
  • A validated 301 redirect map covering all 1,601 current URLs, delivered as a CSV and tested both programmatically and manually before cutover.
  • A content governance playbook assigning ownership, update cadences, approval workflows, and archival triggers for every content type, so the university does not return to 1,600 ungoverned URLs within three years.
  • 40 to 60 block patterns covering every editorial layout the university needs, each one documented with usage guidelines and accessibility specifications.
  • ACF field groups for Programs, Faculty, and Events with required and optional fields, validation, and editor help text embedded in the admin interface.
  • A post-launch SEO monitoring dashboard tracking organic traffic, ranking shifts, AI citation appearances, and Core Web Vitals for the first 90 days after launch.
B.7

Stakeholder management

University websites involve more content stakeholders than almost any other kind of project. Four academic schools, enrollment management, student affairs, campus ministry, athletics, advancement, IT, the president's office, and marketing all have legitimate content needs and strong opinions. Left unmanaged, the result is scope creep, design by committee, and a site that tries to say everything and communicates nothing. Our governance structure is below.

Project governance structure and cadences
Body Composition Cadence Authority
Steering Committee 5 to 7 members Biweekly Binding decisions on scope, timeline, design direction, and content priorities
Content Working Groups One liaison per school or division Monthly Review templates, provide content, validate program accuracy
Escalation protocol Any two disagreeing stakeholders 48-hour SLA Disagreement documented with both positions, resolved by Steering Committee
Feedback windows All reviewers on a milestone 5 business days On-time feedback incorporated; late feedback enters Phase 2 backlog

Rick Mitchell is the single point of consolidation for the university's feedback on behalf of the digital team. Feedback flows through Rick, is logged in the project tracker, and enters the next review cycle. Hallway conversations and direct emails to individual developers do not override structured decisions, and we will politely redirect them back into the process. This is not bureaucracy. It is how we keep the project from entering an infinite revision loop while respecting every stakeholder's right to be heard.

Content visibility audit, early in discovery. Stakeholder conflict in higher education almost always stems from a fear of invisibility: departments worry that the redesign will bury their content. We address this proactively by showing every department, in week three, where their content currently lives, how much traffic it gets, and where it moves to in the new IA. Anxiety gets replaced with data, and the conversations that follow are about priorities instead of panic.

B.8

Collaborative development process

The RFP explicitly asks how we will work with Rick Mitchell's internal team. This is not a client that needs to be educated about web development. Rick writes Drupal and React code himself, maintains an internal design system, and holds a Cornell UX Design certificate. Our job is to extend the team's capacity, not replace it, and our collaboration practices are built for that.

  • Shared Pantheon repo access from day one. Rick's team has full read access to the project repository from the first commit. They see every branch, every pull request, every review comment. Access is native to Pantheon's Git-based infrastructure, not an add-on.
  • Automated CI checks on every pull request. PHPCS, axe-core, Lighthouse CI, visual regression, and link checking run before a human ever reviews the code. Rick's team sees the same green checks we do.
  • Senior developer code review. Every pull request is reviewed by a senior developer before merge, checking for security, performance, accessibility, and component-library adherence.
  • Rick Mitchell's team invited to review. Not required, but encouraged, especially on custom components and integration points. Their comments carry the same weight as ours.
  • Weekly 30-minute technical sync. Between our lead developer and Rick's team: what was built this week, what is coming next, what technical decisions need input. The details that matter for long-term maintainability live in these calls.
  • Documentation written alongside code. Every custom component, every ACF field group, every custom query, and every integration point is documented in a project wiki as it is built, not after the fact.

Knowledge transfer program: 8 hands-on training sessions. WordPress administration for editors and IT. Content editing with the block editor for marketing and departmental content owners. Design system usage for Rick's team. Theme customization for Rick's team. Custom Post Types and ACF for Rick's team. Pantheon workflow (Dev, Test, Live, Multidev) for Rick's team and IT. Analytics and reporting for the marketing team. Accessibility maintenance for editors and Rick's team. Every session uses the university's actual site, not generic examples, and every session is recorded and accompanied by written reference guides that become the university's permanent property.
