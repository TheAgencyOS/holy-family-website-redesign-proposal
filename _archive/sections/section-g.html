Section G

Why us

Several agencies will respond to this RFP. Each of them is capable. The question for Holy Family University is which one is built for the specific job: a WordPress-first migration off Drupal 10, on Pantheon, for a two-person internal team that will own the site for the next five years. This section is our honest answer.

G.1

Unique qualifications

The RFP states a preference for WordPress, and that preference is correct. For Rick Mitchell's team, WordPress offers the largest talent pool to hire from, the most mature plugin ecosystem for the integrations this project needs, and the lowest total cost of ownership across a five-year horizon. We are a WordPress-first agency. We are not a Drupal shop writing a WordPress proposal to win the work, and we are not a generalist firm treating WordPress as a secondary practice. WordPress is the platform we build on every day, and Pantheon is the hosting environment we deploy to for higher education clients.

Qualification 01

AI-accelerated content migration

Five specific stages, not a black box: content audit, semantic redirect mapping, schema mapping, content transformation, and post-migration QA. The audit classifies all 1,601 URLs by LLM batch process, clusters duplicates, and flags stale content. Semantic redirect mapping generates vector embeddings for every source and destination page and auto-accepts matches above a 0.98 similarity threshold. The outcome: 1,601 URLs reduce to approximately 1,200, and the 23 duplicate request-information forms collapse to one smart form routed to Slate by program. AI drafts, humans decide, tests verify. No content goes live without human review.

Qualification 02

Consolidation as strategy, not cost cutting

910 URLs (57 percent of the sitemap) have not been updated since 2024 or earlier. 654 news articles account for 41 percent of the sitemap, most of them older than two years and invisible to AI search. 54 magazine URLs are split across two conflicting prefixes, /magazine/ (32) and /magazines/ (22), fragmenting both analytics and authority. Archiving these is not corner cutting. It is the information architecture decision that makes every other metric on the site better: faster migration, tighter IA, stronger SEO signal concentration on the pages that drive enrollment, and a site Rick Mitchell's team can actually govern.

Qualification 03

AI Engine Optimization built in

Half of prospective students use AI tools weekly to research academic programs, and 93 percent of AI search sessions end without a click. The content the AI surfaces is the first impression. AI-referred visitors who do click through convert at 4.4 times the rate of traditional organic traffic. We build every program page as the definitive answer for its topic: FAQPage schema (the highest citation probability of any structured data type), EducationalOccupationalProgram on program pages, llms.txt at the domain root (currently missing from holyfamily.edu, a concrete day-one win), and a content freshness cadence that keeps priority pages inside the two-month citation window.

Source: industry research on AI search behavior, 2024, aggregated in our competitive analysis.

Qualification 04

WCAG 2.2 AA, shifted left across every phase

The current homepage scores Lighthouse Accessibility 96. That number is misleading. Lighthouse runs axe-core automated rules, which catch only 30 to 57 percent of WCAG failures. Focus order, landmarks, managed focus, reading order, labels for custom controls, and live regions all require human review. The scan also does not exercise program pages, the 23 RFI forms, Slate integration, or the 654 news articles. We shift accessibility left into design (contrast verified at 4.5:1, 24px touch targets in wireframes), development (axe-core in CI, semantic HTML enforced), content (WYSIWYG guardrails, alt text required before publish), and QA (manual keyboard and screen reader testing on every critical user flow).

G.5

Questions or clarifications

The RFP explicitly invites questions, and the quality of the questions an agency asks at the proposal stage is usually a fair predictor of the quality of the work that follows. Here are ours. We would like to discuss them during Discovery.

  • Drupal Paragraphs usage. What is the current Paragraphs usage pattern across program pages, news articles, and landing pages? This is the single biggest technical risk factor for migration complexity, and the answer determines how much of the content model translates cleanly to ACF and Gutenberg block patterns.
  • Pantheon Autopilot today. Is the university currently using Pantheon Autopilot for Drupal core and module updates, and if so, what is the cadence? The answer tells us how much of the WebOps muscle memory is already in place versus what needs to be built.
  • Integration surface beyond Slate. Beyond Technolutions Slate, what other systems require integration: student information system, learning management system, course catalog feed, campus maps, event calendar, giving platform? Each integration has its own scope, risk, and plugin decision.
  • Request-information form strategy. What is the intended path for the 23 existing Slate-sourced request-information forms? Replace with a single smart form that routes to Slate by program, or preserve the legacy forms in parallel during migration and retire them on a defined timeline?
  • PDF remediation scope. Is PDF remediation in scope? If so, what is the approximate page count of legacy PDFs (admissions materials, academic catalogs, policy documents) and the target compliance level? PDF remediation is typically scoped as a post-launch phase, but we want to confirm the expectation before sizing the engagement.
