Section D

Timeline and Project Management

We kick off June 1, 2026 and target a March 1, 2027 launch across five phases and ten months of active work. The schedule is realistic because Holy Family has already done the internal preparation that usually slows discovery, because our content consolidation reduces migration scope from 1,601 URLs to roughly 1,200, and because we run content tooling, component development, and migration in parallel rather than in series.

D.1

Proposed timeline

Kickoff is June 1, 2026. Target go-live is March 1, 2027, with a launch window that extends through April 2027. That gives us a 4 to 5 week buffer inside Phase 5 for any issues that surface during user acceptance testing. A Monday launch is preferred so the team has a full business week of post-launch monitoring before the weekend.

Decision-making framework

Every decision on this project falls into one of three tiers, each with a defined owner and response window.

  • Tier 1, routine. CSS details, plugin selection within approved categories, content formatting conventions. Resolved by the project team, documented in the weekly status note. No university approval required.
  • Tier 2, design and content. Navigation structure, page layout choices, content consolidation calls, form field definitions, homepage hierarchy. We present a recommendation with rationale. The university responds within 3 business days. If no response, we proceed with the recommendation and log it in writing.
  • Tier 3, strategic and contractual. Scope changes, budget adjustments, timeline changes, CMS architecture changes, integration additions, launch date changes. Written impact analysis, joint meeting with Rick Mitchell and Katherine Primus, formal written approval required before work proceeds.

The five approval gates

  • G1: Discovery complete. Content model, information architecture, and technical approach approved. Rick Mitchell and Katherine Primus sign off at the end of Phase 1.
  • G2: Design approved. Visual design, component library, and responsive layouts approved. Rick Mitchell and Katherine Primus sign off at the end of Phase 2.
  • G3: Development complete. Feature-complete site on the Dev environment, functionality verified. Rick Mitchell signs off at the end of Phase 3.
  • G4: Content and testing complete. Migrated content, functional and cross-browser QA, and WCAG 2.2 AA conformance report accepted. Rick Mitchell and Katherine Primus sign off at the end of Phase 4.
  • G5: Launch authorization. UAT closed, training delivered, launch readiness checklist clean. Katherine Primus or an executive designee signs off at the end of Phase 5.
D.4

Change management

Projects at this scope will encounter scope changes. Our job is not to prevent every change but to make sure every change is intentional, documented, and understood before work begins. The process has four steps: a change request is submitted with a description of what is changing and why; we deliver a written impact assessment within 2 to 3 business days covering scope, timeline, and budget dimensions; the request is approved under the tier rules below; and the approved change is logged, the plan is updated, and the next weekly status reflects it.

Change request approval tiers
Change typeImpactApprover
No impact CR No change to budget or critical path Rick Mitchell
Minor timeline CR Timeline impact of 1 week or less, no budget change Rick Mitchell
Budget or major timeline CR Budget change, or timeline impact greater than 1 week Rick Mitchell and Katherine Primus, or designated budget authority

We recommend reserving 10 to 15 percent of the project budget as a change contingency. This lets the project absorb reasonable scope adjustments (an additional integration, a new template, a content rewrite) without triggering a separate procurement for each one. Any unused contingency is returned at project close.

D.5

Quality assurance across phases

Quality is a practice embedded in every phase, not a bolt-on at the end. Automated checks run on every commit, accessibility is reviewed at the mockup stage, and every pull request gets a peer code review before it reaches the Dev environment.

Quality assurance activities by phase
PhaseQA activities
Discovery Content model review against three real content examples per post type; information architecture validation via tree testing
Design Accessibility review of all mockups: contrast ratios, touch targets, focus state design, heading hierarchy; responsive review at four breakpoints
Development Automated CI checks (axe-core, Lighthouse, HTML validation, broken link checks), peer code review on every pull request, biweekly QA sprint reviews
Migration and Testing Dedicated QA cycle covering functional, cross-browser, accessibility, and performance testing on the full site
Launch User acceptance testing, regression testing, 72-hour post-launch monitoring

Phase 4 testing detail

  • Functional testing across every page template, every Gravity Form, every integration, every content type, and every user role, against written acceptance criteria with pass or fail evidence for each.
  • Cross-browser testing on Chrome, Firefox, Safari, and Edge across Windows and macOS; mobile testing on iOS Safari and Android Chrome; responsive verification at 320, 768, 1024, and 1440 pixel breakpoints on real devices and BrowserStack for combinations not available in-house.
  • Accessibility testing in three layers: automated axe-core scans across the full site, manual keyboard-only navigation and screen reader testing with VoiceOver on macOS and iOS plus NVDA on Windows, and recommended assistive-technology user sessions during UAT. Deliverable: a WCAG 2.2 AA conformance report with pass, fail, or partial status for every tested criterion, suitable as the basis for an ACR or VPAT.

Risk monitoring

Our full risk register (Drupal Paragraphs complexity, stakeholder alignment delays, content review bottlenecks, integration surprises, and enrollment-season launch pressure) is documented in Section B.9 with likelihood, impact, and mitigation for each item. One risk deserves explicit callout here because it shapes how Phase 4 is sequenced.

Holiday schedule compression

Phase 4 runs from Nov 30, 2026 through Jan 22, 2027, which overlaps the university's winter break. We have deliberately structured weeks 1 to 3 around the automated bulk migration, redirect mapping, and machine-assisted content transformation work that requires minimal university involvement. University-dependent activities (content review, accessibility sign-off, UAT preparation) are scheduled for January when staff return. The holiday window becomes a feature of the schedule rather than a hazard.
