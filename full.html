Executive Summary

What we will build, and why

Holy Family University has outgrown the site it is running on. We will replace it with a WordPress platform on Pantheon that Rick Mitchell's team can edit, measure, and extend without calling us, built to WCAG 2.2 AA from the first wireframe, and tied into Slate so every inquiry lands where enrollment already lives.

The challenge

A 1,601-page site that is failing the people it is meant to serve

Our April 10 scrape of holyfamily.edu returned 1,601 URLs, not the "approximately 1,300" cited in the RFP. Of those, 654 news articles (41 percent of the sitemap) still sit in the index, most of them years old. 910 URLs (57 percent of the site) have not been updated since 2024 or earlier. 23 duplicate request-for-information forms are scattered across program pages, each one a fork in the road for a prospective student and a point of drift from Katherine Primus, VP for Marketing and Communications, and her marketing system. Lighthouse 12.8.2 clocked the homepage at a 21.19 second Largest Contentful Paint on mobile and a Performance score of 51, numbers that do not describe a site a nursing applicant will wait out on a phone between clinicals. Accessibility scored 96 on automated rules, but the ADA Title II rule that took effect on April 24, 2024 and the university's ongoing Section 504 obligations hold public universities to the full WCAG 2.1 AA standard, not just what axe-core can see. The gap the homepage scan does not exercise, the 23 RFI forms, the program pages, and the 654 news articles, is where the real exposure lives.

B.1

Project approach

Three principles organize every decision on this engagement, and they resolve the tradeoffs that will come up in week four and week twenty the same way. Rick Mitchell, Director of Digital Experience Design, writes code himself and maintains Holy Family University's internal design system. Katherine Primus, VP for Marketing and Communications, owns the budget and the enrollment targets it defends. The principles below are the ones we will reach for when their two vantage points pull in different directions.

Enrollment is the organizing metric. Not the only metric, but the tiebreaker. When two good options compete, we pick the one that makes it easier for a prospective student to find the right program, understand its value, and take the next step. Program discovery, information requests, and application starts get the most rigorous design attention, the fastest load times, and the clearest calls to action. Current students, alumni, faculty, and donors are served through defined pathways that do not compete with enrollment conversion.

Content strategy precedes visual design. The current site has 1,601 indexed URLs across 180 plus distinct top-level sections. That is evidence of organic growth without centralized governance, and a new visual layer applied to that structure would produce a better-looking version of the same navigational complexity. The audit drives the information architecture. The information architecture drives the wireframes. The wireframes drive the visual design. No reordering.

The site we build is the site your team runs. Rick Mitchell's team will own this website for the next five or more years. Every component, every content model, every workflow is designed for maintainability by a lean internal team, not for ongoing agency dependency. Documented component library, content templates with guardrails, structured knowledge transfer. When we leave, your team should be faster and more capable than it was before we arrived.

B.2

Discovery and strategy methodology

University websites fail when departments feel unheard and succeed when departments feel represented inside a coherent structure. Discovery is six weeks of structured listening, synthesis, and research before a single wireframe is drawn, run in three phases with a documented handoff at the end of each one.

1

Institutional listening (weeks 1 to 2)

Structured interviews and workshops with executive sponsors, the academic deans of the four schools (Arts and Sciences, Business and Technology, Education, Nursing and Health Sciences), enrollment and admissions, student services, Rick Mitchell's digital and IT team, and student representatives. Each session runs 60 to 90 minutes with a pre-session questionnaire and a written summary delivered within 48 hours for validation. The goal is an auditable record of stakeholder input that prevents scope disputes later.

2

Synthesis and prioritization (week 3)

A scored Stakeholder Findings Report maps every surfaced need against three criteria: enrollment impact, implementation feasibility, and audience reach. Needs that score high on all three become core requirements. Needs that score high on impact but low on feasibility enter the Phase 2 backlog. Needs that score low on impact are documented and deferred. Every stakeholder sees why their request landed where it did.

3

Content strategy and research (weeks 4 to 6)

Analytics deep dive on the existing GA4, Crazy Egg, and Hotjar stack. Competitive analysis against three named peers. Task-based user research with real prospective and current students. The full 1,601-URL content audit begins and runs in parallel through visual design, because the audit is the single largest workstream in the project and it cannot wait.

B.3

Information architecture approach

The current site has more than 180 distinct top-level URL sections. That is not navigation, it is a filing cabinet with no drawers. The fact that /nursing silently redirects to /grad-nursing is the kind of IA debt that accumulates when departments can create top-level URLs without architectural review: a program accidentally renames itself at the URL level, the old URL is kept as a redirect, and ten years later nobody remembers why. Our first job is to rationalize that structure into eight to twelve primary sections that a prospective student can scan and a content editor can maintain.

B.4

Design process

Design begins with evidence, not mood boards. By the time a designer opens Figma, we have stakeholder interviews, usability findings on the current site, analytics behavior, and a validated IA in hand. The process then moves through four phases, each one gated by a tangible artifact the Steering Committee signs off on.

1

Low-fidelity wireframes

Grayscale layouts for 8 to 10 key page templates focused on content hierarchy, component placement, and conversion flow. No color, no imagery. This is where we validate structure without aesthetic distraction.

2

High-fidelity mockups

Full visual design applied to approved wireframes. Two design directions for the homepage and one direction for interior pages, all built from the same component system. Typography, color, photography, and micro-interactions are all resolved here.

3

Interactive prototypes

Clickable prototypes for the three most critical user journeys: program discovery to RFI submission, admissions information to application start, and homepage to campus visit request. These prototypes are tested with 5 to 8 users before any production code is written.

4

Design QA

Our designers review every implemented component against approved mockups throughout development, flagging deviations before they compound. Design QA is not a phase at the end. It is a continuous checkpoint that runs alongside every sprint.

Respecting Rick Mitchell's design system. Rick has already invested in an internal design system. We will not discard that work. Our sequence is audit, translate, extend, then document, in that order. We audit what exists, translate the tokens and patterns that carry over into the new WordPress block theme, extend the library to cover the components the new site needs, and document everything in a Figma file that mirrors the production code. We do not replace a design system that is already working.

The mobile-first discipline is non-negotiable. Over 60 percent of prospective student traffic arrives on a phone. We design for the small screen first and enhance for larger viewports, rather than designing for desktop and then compressing. Touch targets meet the WCAG 2.2 minimum. Form fields use the correct input types for mobile keyboards. Critical conversion paths (Request Info, Apply, Visit) are reachable within one thumb tap from any page. If a content block does not work on a phone screen, it does not ship.

B.5

Development methodology

We build on WordPress using modern practices that produce maintainable, performant, and secure code, and every technical standard is enforced in the CI pipeline so nothing ships that silently bypasses the rules.

  • WordPress Coding Standards enforced via PHPCS with the WordPress-Extra ruleset on every commit.
  • Semantic HTML5 as the structural foundation. Proper heading hierarchy, landmark regions, native form elements, and ARIA attributes only where native semantics are insufficient.
  • Utility-first CSS compiled from design tokens. No specificity wars, no !important overrides, and a single source of truth for color, type, spacing, and motion values.
  • Progressive enhancement JavaScript. Core functionality works without JS. Enhanced interactions (mega-menu, form validation, lazy loading, scroll spy) layer on top.
  • PHP 8.2 plus with strict typing where applicable, following WordPress Plugin API conventions for all custom functionality.
  • Git-based workflow on Pantheon. Feature branches, pull requests with mandatory code review, automated testing on merge, promotion through Dev, Test, and Live environments.

How it is built

Every technology choice in this section answers one question: can Rick Mitchell's team operate, extend, and defend this site for five years without an agency on retainer. C.1

CMS recommendation: WordPress

We recommend traditional, full-stack WordPress as the content management system for the redesigned Holy Family University site. Not headless WordPress. Not a decoupled React or Next.js front end. The same WordPress that powers roughly 43 percent of the web and the majority of private universities that have migrated off Drupal in the past five years. This recommendation is grounded in three factors specific to a 3,600 plus student institution with a lean web team.

1. Editorial usability for a small team. Rick Mitchell runs a two-person web practice. The CMS has to earn its place for them, not against them. WordPress ships with the Gutenberg block editor, real-time preview, in-context editing, and a media library that editors actually use without training wheels. Drupal's editorial surface has improved in versions 10 and 11, but WordPress is still the benchmark for content author experience, and the gap widens when you measure it in "hours saved per publish."

2. The plugin and developer ecosystem is unmatched. WordPress has the largest active developer community of any CMS, which matters for the practical moment when Holy Family University needs to hire a developer, scope a specific integration, or hand off a bug to a freelancer. WordPress talent is abundant and competitively priced. Drupal talent is thinner, pricier, and concentrated in a shrinking number of shops. Across a five-year ownership horizon, that difference compounds into real dollars and real scheduling relief.

3. Lower total cost of ownership over 5 years. WordPress core is free. The premium tools this project needs (ACF Pro, Gravity Forms Elite, Rank Math Pro, a managed Pantheon plan) total under $1,000 per year in licensing. Drupal's modules are also free, but the development and maintenance labor to reach equivalent functionality is consistently higher, as is the cost of every future Drupal major version upgrade. For an institution operating under fiscal discipline (S and P A minus, stable outlook), WordPress delivers more capability per dollar.

Why not headless

Four concrete reasons we do not recommend headless WordPress for Holy Family University
  1. Editorial workflow degrades. In a headless setup the preview, in-context editing, and WYSIWYG experience that make WordPress intuitive all require custom re-engineering. Your editors lose the ability to see what a page looks like before publishing unless we rebuild that capability from scratch, on your budget, and maintain it forever.
  2. The plugin ecosystem breaks. Gravity Forms, Rank Math, accessibility checkers, GTM integration, block patterns, and every future plugin you might want all assume they render the page themselves. In a headless architecture, each one needs a front-end reimplementation or a replacement, and the "works on day one" list shrinks to nothing.
  3. It creates a permanent skills gap. A headless front end requires ongoing React or Next.js expertise. Your internal strength is WordPress, PHP, and content. Headless introduces a framework your team cannot maintain independently, which permanently re-ties you to an agency. That is the opposite of the outcome you asked for.
  4. Two codebases instead of one. Headless means maintaining a WordPress back end plus a separate front-end application, each with its own dependencies, deploy pipelines, and security updates. For a site that needs to be stable for five years on a lean budget, this doubles the surface area for bugs and technical debt.

Future-proofing without headless. We model content in Advanced Custom Fields (ACF) Pro with full REST API exposure from day one. Every custom post type, every field group, every taxonomy is reachable over the WordPress REST API. If Holy Family University ever needs to serve content to a mobile app, a digital signage system, an admissions kiosk, or a future decoupled front end, the content layer is already an API. You get the editorial experience of traditional WordPress today and the read-API flexibility of headless the moment you need it, without paying for headless up front.

C.4

Accessibility compliance: WCAG 2.2 AA

The legal landscape. The ADA Title II Final Rule, published in April 2024, required public universities to meet WCAG 2.1 AA by April 24, 2026. That deadline has passed. As a recipient of federal funds, Holy Family University is independently subject to Section 504 and Section 508, which impose the same standard. We build to WCAG 2.2 AA, one version ahead of the legal floor. WCAG 2.2 is fully backward-compatible with 2.1, so meeting 2.2 automatically satisfies the current legal requirement and provides a compliance margin for the next regulatory update.

The six new success criteria introduced in WCAG 2.2 and the implementation approach for each
Criterion Requirement How we address it
2.4.11 Focus Not Obscured (AA) Keyboard focus must not be hidden behind sticky headers, modals, or other overlapping elements. Focus management designed into every component. Sticky header includes a focus offset; dialogs use scroll padding and scroll-margin-top so the focused element is never obscured.
2.5.7 Dragging Movements (AA) Any drag interaction must have a single-pointer alternative (click or tap). No drag-only interactions in the design. Sliders, reorder controls, and map interactions all expose standard click, tap, and keyboard paths.
2.5.8 Target Size Minimum (AA) Interactive targets must be at least 24 by 24 CSS pixels. Component library enforces a 44 by 44 pixel minimum touch target, which exceeds the 24 pixel floor and satisfies Apple HIG and WCAG 2.5.5 AAA as well.
3.3.8 Accessible Authentication (AA) Login cannot require cognitive function tests (puzzle CAPTCHAs); must allow password managers and paste. All forms support autofill and paste. Spam protection uses honeypot fields and reCAPTCHA v3 (score-based, invisible), never puzzle CAPTCHAs.
3.2.6 Consistent Help (A) Help contact links appear in the same location across pages. The global header and footer include a persistent "Contact" and "Help" link set, enforced by a shared template part rendered on every page.
3.3.7 Redundant Entry (A) Do not require users to re-enter information they have already provided in the same process. Multi-step Gravity Forms carry values forward between steps. The Request for Information flow auto-populates known Slate Ping cookie data where consented.

Shift left. Most agencies treat accessibility as a QA checkpoint: build first, audit later, fix under deadline pressure. That is how sites ship with 200 open violations. We shift accessibility left into every phase. Design: color contrast validated before a pixel is placed, touch targets sized in wireframes, focus states designed as a first-class visual. Development: semantic HTML and ARIA from the first line of code, axe-core linting in the IDE and in the CI pipeline, pull requests that introduce regressions cannot merge. Content: editor guardrails enforce alt text, heading hierarchy, and link purpose. QA: manual keyboard-only and screen reader testing with NVDA, JAWS, and VoiceOver on the templates that automated tools cannot validate. Accessibility is not a phase; it runs alongside everything else.

Common university website failures we prevent

  • Missing alt text (WCAG 1.1.1). Enforced at the editor level. Images cannot be inserted without an alt value (or an explicit "decorative" flag that writes alt="").
  • Insufficient color contrast (WCAG 1.4.3). Palette validated in the design system to 4.5:1 for body text and 3:1 for large text and UI components before a pixel ships.
  • Keyboard navigation failures (WCAG 2.1.1). Every interactive component tested with keyboard only. No interaction is mouse exclusive.
  • Missing video captions (WCAG 1.2.2). Wistia (already in your stack) supports captions natively; captioning is a content requirement in the governance doc, not a nice-to-have.
  • Inaccessible PDFs (WCAG 1.3.1). Accessible PDF templates provided for new documents; legacy PDF remediation scoped as a post-launch phase, not buried in launch scope.
  • Form labeling failures (WCAG 3.3.2). Every Gravity Forms field carries a programmatic label, explicit error messaging, and instructions. No placeholder-as-label patterns.
