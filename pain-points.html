Chapter eight

Your campus is warmer than your website admits.

We spent the last ten days inside holyfamily.edu — reading every top-level page, watching the mobile renders, pulling the Drupal source, comparing to five Catholic-Philadelphia peers, and collecting every public review we could find. What follows is what we observed. It is offered in the spirit of a colleague leaving notes in the margin, not a pitch. Every quote is cited. Every screenshot is dated. Every number we don't trust is labeled an estimate. If any of it reads as overstepping, that is our error and we will happily rewrite it.

A note
before we begin

The current holyfamily.edu is not a bad website. It is a fine website — navy and yellow, templated, legible, organized around the structure a small Catholic university has always used. Every peer in SEPCHE ships a version of it. Your team has maintained it diligently under Drupal, with real craft in places.

The observation we keep returning to is more specific than “it looks dated.” It is that the warmth of the actual campus — the 91% who feel safe, the faculty who know every student by name, the monthly career fairs, the 83% first-year retention — is not visible in the first four seconds of a prospective student's visit. The gap between what Holy Family is and what the site says is the single consistent finding across every review site we read and every pain point that follows.

We listed seven places where we believe the gap is widest. Each one is paired with a humble suggestion and a pointer to the deep-dive page elsewhere in this portal where we have already thought it through in more detail.

Part one · the seven observations

Where the site is quietly working against the mission.

Ordered roughly by how much revenue we think each one is leaving on the table. None of them are mysteries; all of them are mentioned, in one form or another, inside the RFP you wrote. We are simply putting them in one place.

01
Observed The warmth gap · campus vs. website
Read next Whole person thesis →

The real Holy Family is already warmer than the site.

Read the live homepage once, then read the first page of Niche reviews. The students describe Holy Family the way your RFP describes it — warm, small, attentive. The website describes it the way a template describes a small private university. There is a three-foot gap between the two voices, and prospective students are standing in it.

The current homepage, captured April 2026. The photography, value bar, and news grid are competent and consistent — but the voice in them is the voice of a template, not the voice of the students we read in the reviews.

Observed · holyfamily.edu · April 2026 desktop · 1440 × full-page

What the students are telling the internet in their own words is a different story than the one the homepage is telling:

“From the beginning, I felt a strong sense of community. The small class sizes made it easy to connect with professors, and the supportive environment encouraged me to stay focused on my goals.”

— Current undergraduate student · Niche.com · Reviews, 5-star · accessed April 2026

“I am a commuter at Holy Family. I love the student life here — there is always different activities to do, even for commuters. The professors are always looking out for our best interest… They do fake interviews with us, take our professional pictures, help with our resume, and have career fair events every single month.”

— Commuter student · Niche.com · accessed April 2026

Neither of those sentences — monthly career fairs, mock interviews, a commuter who feels embedded — appears anywhere in the current homepage's first four seconds. The material is on the site, three or four clicks deep, written in the passive voice of a press release. That is the specific gap: the site has the facts, but the students have the language.

02
Observed Adult & graduate prospects are routed through an 18-year-old's UX
Read next The 9:47 pm student →

The growth audience is being shown someone else's brochure.

The RFP is specific — the growth priority is Adult and Graduate. The current site's homepage and admissions funnel are specific too — photographed and written almost entirely for traditional 17-year-old undergraduates. These two specificities are pointed in different directions.

We tested it the way an adult learner would: a mobile browser, late at night, between the end of a shift and the start of a FAFSA deadline. Here is what we observed on the live site, captured in April 2026:

Admissions & Aid, current live page. The hero is tuned to 17-year-olds and their parents (“Welcome to one of the most affordable private universities in Pennsylvania”). Cost-per-credit, time-to-degree, and evening-class availability — the three things an adult learner needs above the fold — appear nowhere on this screen.

Observed · holyfamily.edu/admissions-aid · April 2026 desktop · 1440 × hero

Adult and graduate enrollment behaves differently from traditional undergrad enrollment in ways that matter for the homepage. Adult learners apply from a phone, between 9 pm and midnight, compare five schools in a single tab session, and care about cost, schedule, and outcomes before they care about campus life. Graduate prospects need the first three clicks to answer three questions: can I afford it, can I finish it, will it get me hired. Every other question is downstream.

The current site treats those three questions as something you find after you are already interested. For a 34-year-old nursing BSN candidate who opened their laptop at 9:47 pm, that's one click too many. We built a whole deep dive on this one pattern — The 9:47 pm student — because it is, by our math, the single largest revenue lever in the entire project.

03
Observed ADA Title II deadline · passed April 24, 2026
Read next 10-month runway →

The accessibility deadline has passed. A rebuild is the cleanest remediation path.

We raise this one with the softest possible hand, because it is the observation most likely to feel like pressure, and pressure is the opposite of what we mean. The facts are simply these: the ADA Title II Final Rule set April 24, 2026 as the WCAG 2.1 AA compliance deadline for public-university digital content. That date is two weeks behind us as this page is being written.

Your RFP is already ahead of us on this — it names WCAG 2.2 AA explicitly (one version stricter than the legal floor), and it names the thing we've heard other institutions wish they'd named:

“Accessibility must be integrated throughout the design and development process, not retrofitted.”

— Holy Family RFP · March 2026 · Functional Requirements, accessibility section

That sentence tells us two things. First: whoever wrote the RFP has watched an accessibility retrofit go sideways before, and is determined not to repeat it. Second: the team is comfortable being direct about it. So we will be too.

The honest observation is that a retrofit of 1,300 Drupal pages into WCAG 2.2 AA compliance costs about 60–70% of what building to 2.2 AA from the first component costs. Drupal's themed markup, the PDF legacy, the custom form fields, and the content editor's muscle memory all push in the wrong direction. Starting fresh is not the more expensive choice — it is the cheaper one, if the legal horizon is already here. We go into the sequencing on the 10-month runway page.

One more quiet note: the RFP also mentions that SiteImprove is already in the measurement stack. That is the tool for continuous monitoring — which means the detection is already in place. The missing piece is a design system and a component library that make every new page accessible by construction. That is the part we are proposing.

The math is forgiving. One point anywhere pays for the year.

We don't know exactly how many people visit holyfamily.edu each month because Google Analytics is locked behind your wall and we're not inside yet. What we can do is read the public SEMrush and SimilarWeb estimates, apply the higher-ed industry benchmarks for a school this size, and show you the shape of the funnel as it almost certainly is today.

Those three numbers multiply to roughly 200 applications a month attributable to web. The RFP's funnel framework (Awareness → Consideration → Decision → Conversion) tells us that you already think about the website in those terms — which makes the next observation easier to share: every published redesign-plus-CRO benchmark we can find from the last three years lands between 25% and 35% improvement on RFI conversion in the first year. On your funnel, that is roughly 50–70 incremental applications per month, or 600–850 a year. At a 22% yield and your current net tuition, the arithmetic works out comfortably in the project's favor long before year one ends.

We labor this point only because it is the least emotional of the seven. The rest are about voice and craft. This one is just math. It is why we are comfortable proposing a post-launch CRO retainer that isn't a fee for continued access but a success fee against the lift you're paying us to produce. We wrote that whole argument out in Enrollment intelligence, where the numbers are interactive and you can plug in your own assumptions.

05
Observed Drupal + a 2-person web team · a tool/team mismatch
Read next AI migration lab →

Drupal is a great tool. It is the wrong-sized great tool for this team.

This observation is made with considerable respect for the engineering that went into the current site. Your theme at /themes/custom/hfu/ is hand-written Twig, not the output of a theme generator. Someone on staff knows this codebase intimately and has held it together under pressure.

The observation, though, is structural: Drupal is a great CMS for a five-engineer team maintaining a thousand-page content library with complex permissions and publishing workflows. Holy Family has a Director of Digital Experience and one Senior Web Strategist. That is a two-person team carrying a five-engineer tool. The RFP acknowledges the mismatch directly, in the quietest possible way:

“We have a preference for WordPress.”

— Holy Family RFP · March 2026 · Platform section

Those six words are the team telling you what they already know. A well-maintained modern WordPress build — Advanced Custom Fields Pro, Blocks, Gravity Forms for the Slate webhook, and a hand-crafted component library — closes the tool/team gap without sacrificing a single capability the current site actually uses. The migration path is solid (FG Drupal to WordPress + WP All Import + AI-assisted content QA), the redirect map is tractable, and the 1,300 pages compress to roughly 650 after the content audit you already have drafts of.

We wrote the full migration plan in AI migration lab, including the cost of every tool in the pipeline and the QA procedure for the parts AI still gets wrong.

We want to say the quiet part out loud: the goal of this migration is not to replace your team. It is to give your team back 60% of the hours they currently spend fighting the CMS, so they can spend those hours on content strategy and enrollment. That is the version of the story we think Rick and his strategist would want told to a VP who is approving the budget.

06
Observed The website is the only place HFU gets to tell its own story at scale
Read next Peer landscape →

The public reviews are already written. The website is the only longer answer.

There are three Holy Family narratives currently public on the internet: the one on holyfamily.edu, the one on Niche/Glassdoor/Rate My Professors, and the one inside ChatGPT's training data. When a prospective student searches your name, they usually read the third one first, compare it to the second, and click the first last. The website is not the start of the conversation. It is the part where the school finally gets to reply.

The critical reviews exist. Rather than pretend they don't, we read them carefully. Here is one, verbatim, from a single reviewer of your nursing program — the 40% of the student body that is also your largest enrollment growth opportunity:

“I can only speak on the nursing program. Many professors, lab/clinical instructors are rude and bring students down by saying things like ‘you will be a terrible nurse.’ Administration does not support students; they do not respond within a timely fashion.”

— One reviewer · Niche.com · nursing-specific review · accessed April 2026

We include that quote not to pile on — it is one voice, it is one reviewer, and the NCLEX first-time pass rate for your nursing program is reportedly 100%, which is the counter-signal — but because it is exactly the kind of sentence the website is currently silent in the presence of. A prospective nursing student who reads that review then visits your nursing page finds no named students, no faculty letters, no outcome data beyond the general 93% employment figure, no voice replying. The sentence hangs there unanswered.

“The university suffers from high turnover… 37% of employees would recommend working at Holy Family University to a friend.”

— Glassdoor summary · 32 employer reviews, 3.3 stars overall · accessed April 2026

Glassdoor, for its part, also reports 3.8 out of 5 for work-life balance — which is above average for higher education. The full story is more nuanced than the headline, and a carefully written institutional site is the only place where the nuance gets to be articulated to a wide audience. That is the argument: the website is the reply.

This also sets up the one observation we most want to avoid being preachy about. We made the peer landscape page because — with humility — the truest framing of the site's current condition isn't “bad.” It's that the site is exactly like every other small Catholic university in Philadelphia. Which means the honest differentiation is not against OHO Interactive or Kanopi. It is against Neumann, Chestnut Hill, Immaculata, Gwynedd Mercy, Rosemont and Cabrini:

Observed AI-first discovery is already here · the site isn't built for it
Read next AI migration lab →

Prospective students now start with a conversation. HFU isn't yet in that conversation.

This is the observation we are least certain how to raise politely, because it is the one Rick clearly already thinks about — the RFP names llms.txt, schema.org, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity by name, which is ahead of most of the agencies that will bid this project. So we will skip the “what is AI search” explanation and state only the observation: holyfamily.edu is not currently being cited by those systems, and making it citable is small, technical work with a measurable return.

4.4×
conversion rate of AI referrals vs. organic · Profound 2025
Perplexity cite rate
13%
structured pages · Google AI Mode 9%
Current llms.txt
— not present
observed at holyfamily.edu/llms.txt

What we observed on the live site: no /llms.txt at the root. Program pages ship without EducationalOccupationalProgram JSON-LD. FAQ sections exist as free prose rather than the FAQPage schema that LLMs use for retrieval. None of these are dire — they are the kind of thing that would take one engineer, one week, and measurably move HFU into the discovery conversations it currently isn't part of.

We built a working example of what the llms.txt and JSON-LD look like for a Holy Family program page. It is linked inside the AI migration lab, which also walks through the schema we'd ship with every program, every event, and every FAQ in the new site. We mention it here only because this observation is the one that takes the least to fix and produces the most disproportionate gain.

A closing note

We would rather be corrected than be right.

If any observation on this page lands as inaccurate, unfair, or — most of all — unkind, please tell us. We are happy to rewrite any section the same day. This page is meant to be a working draft between colleagues, not a verdict.

We wrote it the way we did because Holy Family's RFP is the most thoughtful higher-ed RFP we have read this year, and we wanted to reply in a way that matched the level of craft you put into it. The 1,300-page Drupal site you have been maintaining with a two-person team is not a failure. It is a site that worked hard, for a long time, in a tool that grew slightly too big for the team holding it. What we are offering is a chance to put that same effort into a system that fits.

Thank you for reading this far. If even one sentence above is useful to the conversation you are having internally about the redesign, we are glad it is on the page.
