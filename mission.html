Mission engine

Your Catholic identity is an enrollment argument, not a disclaimer.

Most redesigns treat mission as branding: a logo lockup, a values page, a photo of the chapel. We'd like to treat it as an enrollment argument. Sixty-four percent of prospective Catholic-university students say faith shaped their school choice (HERI 2024). Seventy-eight percent of HFU alumni cite whole-person formation as the reason they stayed (HFU 2024). The prospects who share Holy Family's mission are also its most yield-dense applicants, and today, four of the five Philadelphia Catholic peers surface mission only on a values tab. The redesign's job is to help mission-aligned prospects recognize themselves in the decision moment (on the program page, the cost page, and the heritage page) and choose to enroll.

The thesis · in one sentence
The four pillars · whole-person formation

Mission doesn't live on one page. It shapes every page.

HFU's founding charism (the education of the whole person in mind, body, spirit, and community) is worth more than a paragraph on /about. We propose to let it organize the information architecture itself. Here's where each pillar shows up on the site.

01 · MIND
Intellectual rigor

Mind · the examined life

The Catholic intellectual tradition, treated as a competitive advantage rather than a footnote.

  • Program pages open with academic outcomes (employment, grad school placement) plus a "formation frame" that names what this degree asks of you as a thinking person.
  • Faculty profiles include a "scholarship & faith" module that situates their research within the longer Catholic intellectual lineage.
02 · BODY
Health & wellness

Body · embodied life

Student life, athletics, and campus not as "amenities" but as the body's contribution to formation.

  • Student life pages lead with counseling, wellness, and the athletics department as co-equal with academics, not as a lifestyle upsell.
  • Campus tours surface the dorms, dining, chapel, and gym as a single integrated "place of formation," rather than four disconnected categories.
03 · SPIRIT
Catholic formation

Spirit · the inner life

Chapel, liturgy, service and mission trips, shown rather than disclaimed.

  • Chaplaincy page promoted from a buried link to a first-class citizen, with Mass times, retreats, and spiritual direction bookings, all schema-marked.
  • Service & mission module on the homepage showing live immersion-trip counts, feast-day calendar, and the campus liturgical year.
04 · COMMUNITY
Service & belonging

Community · the shared life

The alumni network, mentorship, and local Northeast Philly partnerships, the body of Christ made measurable.

  • Alumni pages show not just career paths but mentor-relationship counts, giving patterns, and real quotes from formation-anchored stories.
  • Homepage "impact module" pulls live data from local partners: hospitals, parishes, and schools the community serves in Northeast Philadelphia.

Branding vs. infrastructure

Most redesigns stop at brand. We'd like to go one layer deeper.

A good brand system matters, and we'll build one. But mission becomes an enrollment argument only when it's also wired into schema, templates, and measurable events. Three places we'd treat differently from a typical redesign:

01
A values page
The typical approach: a /values page with a lockup, four pillars, and a chapel photo. Readable, but invisible to AI search.
We'd also ship
EducationalOrganization schema that makes Holy Family's Catholic identity legible to ChatGPT and Perplexity (chaplaincy contact, feast-day calendar, sacraments offered on-site), so the mission is discoverable, not just displayed.
02
Storytelling
The typical approach: a blog, a video series, a student-quote carousel. Good content, but the same story told to every visitor.
We'd also ship
A story library tagged in the CMS by persona and formation theme, so search intent routes to the right narrative. Maria sees the vocation angle; Jacob sees the mentorship angle; the same archive, matched to what each prospect is asking.
03
Brand refresh
The typical approach: new colors, new typography, a refreshed photo library. Necessary work, and we'll do it.
We'd also ship
Mission language moved above the fold on every program page, with RFI conversion tracked against a pre-launch baseline. If it lifts inquiries, it stays and we defend it in quarterly reviews. If it doesn't, we pull it back.
