Modernizing Holy Family University’s web presence.

Small College Consulting is honored to present this proposal to Holy Family University. In partnership with the University’s team, we are confident that our approach will not only strengthen the institution’s website and connected marketing channels, but will also position University personnel to manage and evolve the site in the future with little to no external vendor support. A first-pass IA for holyfamily.edu.

The current site comprises 1,601 URLs distributed across more than 180 distinct top-level prefixes. The first-pass recommendation presented here consolidates that inventory into eight sections and approximately 650 active pages, a reduction of roughly sixty percent. Every URL is justified by a primary audience. We anticipate that a portion of this recommendation will evolve during Discovery; the remainder reflects structural decisions we believe will hold.

Executive Summary · for the President's Cabinet Section 08 / 30
The thesis of this response

More applications. Specifically adult and graduate.

Holy Family University graduate outcomes are impressive. Ninety-three percent of recent graduates secure full-time employment within six months. Forty percent of the student body pursues programs in Nursing and Health Sciences. The Sisters of the Holy Family of Nazareth continue to shape what began as a 1954 founding into a vibrant Catholic learning community. Closing that distance is the central purpose of this proposal. The conservative analysis presented on page sixteen projects a $2.5M year-one return on the $145K investment.

The RFP's loudest signal · Adult & Graduate enrollment Section 09 / 30

Four tactics for adult and graduate enrollment.

Section 4 of the University's RFP identifies adult and graduate enrollment as the top growth priority. These applicants typically browse from phones in the evening between shifts and need cost and scheduling information to be visible above the fold. The four tactics described below are concrete moves this engagement will ship, not observations about the audience itself.

Move 01 · Decision info above the fold
Tuition, schedule, time-to-degree on the hero.
No "request info to see pricing." No gated PDFs. Every adult and graduate program page will display total cost, per-credit cost, part-time time-to-completion, and career outcomes before any scroll. Projected lift: +25–35% application-start rate.
Move 02 · One smart RFI form
23 duplicates collapse to one.
The current site contains twenty-three separate request-for-information forms. These will be consolidated into a single Gravity Form with program routing, conditional questions, and real-time delivery to Slate via webhook. Projected lift: +15–20% inquiry volume.
Move 03 · Variant B adult hero
A homepage drafted for them.
Page 11 presents the drafted adult and graduate hero. The headline reads "Finish what you started. On your schedule," with credit for prior learning, time-to-degree, and cost displayed up front. The variant is served conditionally based on UTM source and returning-visitor behavior. Projected lift: +40–60% on adult and graduate paid landing pages.
Move 04 · Slate funnel measurement
From visit to deposit, attributed.
A four-stage GA4 funnel, Slate Ping on every page, cross-domain tracking through the application portal, and a Looker Studio dashboard configured for Katherine Primus's team to read directly. See page 17. Projected outcome: first clear ROI attribution.

All JSON-LD, server-rendered, reachable without JavaScript execution as the RFP requires for agentic browsing.

Copy rewritten for AI engine visibility.

Schema and llms.txt describe the site to AI engines, but the copy on every page still has to read clearly to those engines once they land. Small College Consulting’s proprietary content technology automatically rewrites headings, metadata, and body copy across the entire website for answer-engine optimization (AEO). Each page’s original copy is compared against an AI-optimized rewrite before anything ships; the University’s editors accept, reject, or refine every change.

Section B.2–B.3 · Discovery & IA methodology Section 18 / 30
How we listen before we build

Discovery is six weeks of structured listening.

  1. Ingest the 2025 prep work (week 1). Your content audit, governance guidelines, IA analysis, analytics baselines, and stakeholder surveys become our starting position. Zero rework. Phase 1 closes two weeks earlier than a standard Discovery cycle.
  2. Institutional listening (weeks 1–2). Thirty-minute one-on-ones with Dr. Prisco, Katherine Primus, Rick Mitchell, Mark Green, Edward Wright, and Eric Nelson. Each session recorded, synthesized within 48 hours, validated within 72.
  3. Content audit & AI-assisted classification (weeks 2–3). All 1,601 URLs classified via LLM batch into keep / rewrite / consolidate / archive / delete. Human review on every decision. Auditable worksheet delivered.
  4. Task-based user research (weeks 3–4). 8–10 participants across three segments (prospective undergraduate, prospective adult/graduate, current student). First-click testing on four key tasks: find a program, start an application, look up a professor, find an event.
  5. Information architecture validation (weeks 4–5). Card sort with 15–20 participants. Tree test on the proposed navigation. Baseline comparison against the current IA. The sitemap on page 06 is the first-pass recommendation; Discovery validates it with real users.
  6. Content model & sign-off (weeks 5–6). WordPress custom post types, ACF field groups, taxonomies. Drupal-to-WordPress field mapping. Gate G1 closes the phase with Rick Mitchell and Katherine Primus signing off.

The real scope is smaller · the real cost is lower

1,601 URLs to approximately 650.

Viewed only at its surface, content migration appears to be a labor cost. Examined more carefully, it is a question of content strategy. A sixty-percent reduction is not a commitment to delete content; it is a commitment to identify the pages that earn their place in the new site and to protect the URL equity of those that do not. AI drafts the initial classifications, the editorial team and University staff make the final decisions, and automated tests verify that nothing breaks along the way.

Collaborative model · 15 percent of the evaluation Section 20 / 30

The University team builds with us, not receives from us.

Four phrases in the RFP all point in the same direction: partners rather than obstacles, working sessions rather than presentations, knowledge transfer throughout the project, and code review from the University's developers. Each reflects a settled preference on the part of the institution. This response addresses each with a concrete ritual rather than an adjective.

Ritual 01 · kickoff day one
Shared GitHub write access.
Not read access, and not at handoff. The University's team will commit alongside ours from week one. Feature branches, pull requests, the issue tracker, and the project board are open to both sides from the outset.
Ritual 02 · every pull request
Code review in both directions.
Every pull request requires a named reviewer from each side. Nothing merges to the main branch without dual sign-off. Institutional context is captured in pull-request comments rather than left to informal memory.
Ritual 03 · every Tuesday
A 45-minute working pair, not a status meeting.
A live working session focused on the most difficult decision of the week. The tradeoff is whiteboarded together and the resulting decision is committed to the repository. Attendees rotate so that institutional knowledge spreads across the full team.
Ritual 04 · week one
The University's 2025 preparation work, ingested.
The content audit, governance guidelines, information-architecture analysis, and analytics baselines completed by the University in 2025 become the starting position for our Discovery. No rework is required. As a result, Phase 1 closes approximately two weeks earlier than a standard Discovery cycle.

Section C.1 · CMS recommendation Section 21 / 30
Traditional WordPress, not headless · honest tradeoff math

WordPress, not headless.

The University's RFP expresses a preference for WordPress and explicitly invites a rationale comparing traditional and headless approaches. For Rick Mitchell's two-person team, which will be responsible for operating this site over the next five years, the traditional approach is the stronger choice on every axis that matters: the largest talent pool, the most mature plugin ecosystem, and the lowest total cost of ownership. We would not recommend an architecture that the University's internal team could not sustainably operate.

Why WordPress
Editorial usability for a small team.
Rick Mitchell's team needs to publish content rather than support tickets. The Gutenberg editor provides real-time preview, in-context editing, and a mature media library. Since the WordPress 6.x release cycle, this editorial environment has set the benchmark for content-author experience among open-source content management systems.
Why WordPress
The largest developer ecosystem.
When the University needs to engage an additional developer in two or three years, WordPress talent is abundant and competitively priced. The Drupal talent pool is smaller and substantially more expensive. Across a five-year horizon, that difference compounds into a meaningful dollar figure.
Why not headless
Editorial workflow degrades.
Real-time preview and in-context editing require costly re-engineering in a headless configuration. Yoast, Gravity Forms, and GTM integration all assume a traditional front end. The University would be left maintaining two codebases and dependent on React and Next.js skills indefinitely.
Future proof
REST API ready.
ACF Pro exposes every custom post type via the REST API from the first day of development. If the University at any point requires a mobile application, digital signage, or a decoupled front end, the content layer will already be headless-ready. The institution incurs the cost of that flexibility only at the point of actual need.

Section C.4 · WCAG 2.2 AA Section 23 / 30
Integrated throughout, not retrofitted

WCAG 2.2 AA, shifted left.

The RFP insists that accessibility be "integrated throughout the design and development process, not retrofitted." We take that language at face value. Automated tools catch between thirty and fifty-seven percent of real WCAG failures; the remainder must be addressed manually, and that is where the majority of accessibility hours on this project will be spent.

  1. Design phase. Color contrast validated to 4.5:1 before mockups ship. 44×44 px touch targets in wireframes. Focus states designed as a first-class visual element for every interactive component.
  2. Development phase. Semantic HTML and ARIA from the first line. axe-core in the CI pipeline. Pull requests with accessibility regressions cannot merge.
  3. Content phase. Editor guardrails enforce alt text, heading hierarchy, link purpose. Editors cannot publish broken accessibility.
  4. QA phase. Manual keyboard-only testing plus screen reader testing with VoiceOver, NVDA, and JAWS on every template. Not just the homepage.
  5. Launch deliverable. ACR/VPAT signed at launch, not a remediation plan. Siteimprove continuous monitoring already in your stack, we configure new dashboards for WCAG 2.2 AA tracking.

Accessibility will not be bolted on at the end.

Anti-pattern 02
The agency will not disappear at launch.
The University's team will be committing code to the shared repository by week three. A day-ninety runbook, recorded training sessions, and a written succession plan are all project deliverables.
Anti-pattern 03
Slide presentations will not substitute for collaboration.
A weekly forty-five-minute working pair will be devoted to the hardest decision of the week. At the finalist presentation, our team will whiteboard a live information-architecture problem alongside the University's team rather than present slides to it.
Anti-pattern 04
The University team will not be treated as an obstacle.
Rick Mitchell writes Drupal and React himself. The engagement model is structured so that he and his team can extend the site during the build rather than receive a finished artifact at handoff.
