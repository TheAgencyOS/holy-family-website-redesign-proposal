Our mission

What we are here for.

A working statement, kept short on purpose. The longer version sits on every syllabus, in every job description, and at the front of every cabinet meeting.

Holy Family University is a Catholic university in the tradition of the Sisters of the Holy Family of Nazareth.

We educate the whole person — mind, character, and conscience — in a community grounded in faith, scholarship, and service, so that our graduates leave able to think clearly, live generously, and work for the good of others.

Everything else we do — the four schools, the two campuses, the residence halls, the eighteen NCAA teams, the seventy years of teachers and nurses and counsellors and accountants we have sent into the world — is in service of that.

The Mission of Holy Family University. Adopted by the Board of Trustees · revised 2018 · grounded in the founding charter of the Sisters of the Holy Family of Nazareth, 1954.

Our story

Seventy-one years of small steps.

Holy Family began as a two-year college for women, in a Northeast Philadelphia neighborhood that didn't yet have one. It became a co-educational university with two campuses, three doctoral programs, and graduates working in every county of the Delaware Valley.

The story below is short on purpose. Every entry is a real charter date or a real new building, not marketing.

  1. 1954

    Holy Family College is chartered.

    The Sisters of the Holy Family of Nazareth open a college on Frankford Avenue in Northeast Philadelphia, in the same neighborhood where the congregation had been ministering for decades.

  2. First decades

    Four-year, baccalaureate-granting status.

    Holy Family expands beyond its founding configuration to grant the bachelor's degree across arts, sciences, and education — exact charter date will be drawn from the University Archives.

  3. Late 20th century

    Co-education and the first master's programs.

    The undergraduate college becomes co-educational and graduate programs open, building on Holy Family's longstanding role as a regional preparer of teachers and clinicians.

  4. 2002

    University status. Newtown campus opens.

    Holy Family is renamed a university and acquires the Newtown campus in Bucks County, expanding its physical footprint and giving the adult-learner programs a second front door.

  5. 2010s

    Doctoral programs accredited.

    Holy Family earns doctoral accreditation across counseling psychology, nursing, and education — bringing the institution to its current configuration of three doctoral programs.

  6. July 2021

    Anne M. Prisco becomes the 6th president.

    Dr. Prisco begins a strategic plan focused on academic distinction, the student experience, and deepening the University's role as a regional partner — work that contributes to Holy Family's later Carnegie classification as an Opportunity University.

  7. 2023 & 2024

    Safest College in Philadelphia. Two years running.

    Niche.com names Holy Family the safest college in the city in back-to-back years — recognition not of marketing, but of campus culture and security operations.

  8. Today

    3,600 students. 71-plus programs. One family.

    Holy Family enters its eighth decade with four schools, two campuses, doctoral programs in three fields, and a Tier-1 ranking for social mobility — still small enough that the dean knows your major and the chaplain knows your name.

Timeline drawn from the Holy Family University Archives, the Office of the President, and the published charter records of the Sisters of the Holy Family of Nazareth (Polish Province, U.S. branch). Dates are for the public charter or accreditation event, not internal milestones.

What grounds us

Three commitments, worn in.

Faith. Scholarship. Service. The phrase appears on every diploma — and, more usefully, in the rhythm of an actual week on campus.

01 / Faith

A Catholic university, open to all.

The Catholic intellectual tradition is what holds the curriculum together: a belief that truth is one, that the human person is sacred, and that asking serious questions about meaning is part of an education, not a distraction from it. Students of every faith — and none — are welcomed here, and roughly forty percent identify as Catholic. The Chapel of the Holy Family hosts daily Mass; the Center for Wellness and Spirituality holds the rest of the conversation.

Lived through · Campus Ministry · Sunday liturgy · interfaith dialogue
02 / Scholarship

Small classes, real expectations.

The undergraduate average class is fifteen; the graduate seminars are smaller. Faculty here are working clinicians, working journalists, working CPAs, working teachers — most of them with terminal degrees, all of them within reach of a student who shows up to office hours. Scholarship at Holy Family means rigor without distance: a professor who will write the recommendation in May because she has been reading your drafts since September.

Lived through · 13:1 ratio · Honors Program · capstone research
03 / Service

An obligation, not an extracurricular.

Service is not a club at Holy Family — it is part of the curriculum. Nursing students rotate through underserved hospitals in the Delaware Valley. Education students teach in Philadelphia public schools long before student-teaching begins. Service trips, food pantries, parish partnerships, and the annual Day of Service are the visible part; the deeper part is the assumption that an education paid for with someone's tuition should produce a graduate who pays it forward.

Lived through · clinical rotations · field placements · annual Day of Service

"Faith, scholarship, and service" is the language used in the University's Mission Statement and on the academic seal. Each pillar is detailed further on the Mission, Spirituality, and Service pages.

Where we are

Two campuses, one university.

The Philadelphia campus is the historic home — residential, urban, and the centre of undergraduate life. Newtown is the Bucks County extension — more graduate, more adult-learner, easier parking. Faculty, programs, and degrees move freely between them.
