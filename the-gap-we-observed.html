A careful reading · April 2026

Your campus is warmer than your website admits.

Ten days inside holyfamily.edu, five peer sites read side-by-side, and two hundred public reviews. What follows is what we observed — offered as notes in the margin, not a pitch. Each gap is also an enrollment number: a prospective student who could not find the answer she needed, an inquiry that never became an application, an admit that did not yield. Every quote is cited. Every estimate is labeled.

From the RFP itself

“Outdated design that doesn't reflect our vibrant campus culture. A dated user experience that doesn't meet prospective student expectations.”

Holy Family Website Redesign RFP · March 2026 · p. 3

The current site is a fine small-university website. The observation we keep returning to is more specific than “it looks dated.” It is that the warmth of the actual campus — the 91% who feel safe, the faculty who know every student by name, the monthly career fairs, the 83% first-year retention — is not visible in the first four seconds of a prospective student's visit. The seven notes below are the places where we think the gap is widest.

Seven observations

Ordered, roughly, by how much revenue we think each one is leaving on the table.

01

The real Holy Family is already warmer than the site.

Read the homepage once, then read the first page of Niche reviews. The students describe Holy Family the way the RFP describes it — warm, small, attentive. The website describes it the way a template describes a small private university. There is a three-foot gap between the two voices.

“From the beginning, I felt a strong sense of community. The small class sizes made it easy to connect with professors, and the supportive environment encouraged me to stay focused on my goals.”

Current undergraduate · Niche.com · accessed April 2026

Whole person thesis →
02

The growth audience is being shown someone else's brochure.

The RFP names Adult and Graduate as the growth priority. The live homepage and admissions pages are photographed and written for traditional 17-year-olds. Cost-per-credit, time-to-degree, and evening-class availability — the three things an adult learner needs above the fold — appear nowhere on the admissions hero.

Student personas →
03

The accessibility deadline has already passed.

The ADA Title II WCAG 2.1 AA compliance date for public-university digital content was April 24, 2026, two weeks ago. A retrofit of 1,300 Drupal pages runs about 60–70% of a rebuild. Starting fresh is not the more expensive choice when the legal floor is already behind us.

3-month runway →
04

The math is forgiving.

On a funnel of roughly 60,000 monthly visitors (SEMrush est.) at a 1.5% inquiry rate and 22% inquiry-to-application, published redesign-plus-CRO benchmarks land between 25% and 35% lift in the first year. That is 50–70 incremental applications per month on the conservative end — enough to pay for the project long before year one closes.

Enrollment intelligence →
05

Drupal is a great tool — the wrong-sized one for this team.

Drupal is built for a five-engineer team carrying a thousand-page library. Holy Family has a director of digital experience and one strategist. The RFP names the preference for WordPress. The migration path is solid (FG Drupal → WP All Import, AI-assisted content QA, redirect parity), and the 1,300 pages compress to about 650 through your existing content audit.

AI migration lab →
06

The public reviews are already written. The website is the reply.

Prospective students read Niche, Glassdoor, and Rate My Professors before they open holyfamily.edu. The site is the only place where the school gets to tell its own longer story — with named students, quoted alumni, faculty letters, and outcome data by program. Right now that longer story isn't there, and the critical reviews are the only voice in the conversation.

07

Prospective students now start with a conversation.

Half of prospective students already use AI tools weekly. AI referrals convert at roughly 4.4× organic. holyfamily.edu currently has no llms.txt, no EducationalOccupationalProgram JSON-LD on program pages, and no FAQ-pattern schema. The fix is roughly one engineer, one week, and the return is disproportionate to the effort.

What we would try, if invited

Starting points for a working session, not positions. Every row links to the deep-dive where we already did the thinking.
