Student personas & enrollment intelligence

Four students. Four arrivals. One website.

A redesign that treats every visitor like an 18-year-old campus-tour prospect will miss the majority of the people Holy Family needs to enroll. Four personas account for almost all of HFU's recruitment mix: the first-year, the transfer, the adult learner, and the graduate or professional student. Each one arrives on the site at a different hour, on a different device, from a different channel, carrying a different question. The redesigned site is built to recognize which one just arrived, serve the answer they came for, and quietly feed that signal into Slate so the admissions team knows who to call first. That is what "enrollment intelligence" means in practice.

The four mistakes the current site makes · across all four personas

Every scene above traces back to one of four decisions we would undo on day one.

Maria's storyboard is the sharpest version of the problem, but the same four decisions hurt Jasmine, Marcus, and Elena too. A slow mobile load is a lost first-year. A hidden price is a lost transfer. A broken RFI form is a lost graduate applicant. Fixing these four things at once lifts all four personas in parallel.

01
It optimizes for one persona and ignores the other three.

The current holyfamily.edu was built for a traditional 17-year-old prospect arriving in daylight with a parent. That's Jasmine, and even for her, it underperforms. For Marcus, Maria, and Elena it's actively hostile: no transfer-credit calculator, no RN-to-BSN track, no graduate program filter. Every pixel assumes the visitor is on a campus tour; three out of four arrivals are not.

The fix
Dedicated paths in the IA for each of the four personas, each tuned to the hour, device, and question they arrive with.
02
It hides the price.

Program pages never show tuition. Not per credit, not per term, not total. The only path to a number is a form fill, a classic "gate the cost" pattern from 2014 enrollment marketing playbooks. Maria and Elena won't fill out four forms in one night; they'll drop HFU from the list. Jasmine's mom won't forward a link her family can't price.

The fix
Price and time-to-degree above the fold on every program page. No exceptions.
03
The RFI form breaks on a phone.

The current Request Info form is a 12-field, three-column layout written for a desktop monitor and never reflowed for mobile. At 390px, fields overlap, the submit button hides behind the iOS keyboard, and validation errors scroll off-screen. That's 71% of HFU's sessions hitting a broken form. It is the single highest-impact fix on the site.

The fix
One smart form. Four fields on mobile. Pre-filled from URL parameters. Slate-wired with a persona tag.
04
There's no path for the impatient.

Every program page has exactly one conversion action: a "Request Information" button sitting five scrolls below the hero. Maria has 30 minutes. Marcus has a lunch break. Elena is between meetings. None of them want to start a conversation; they want to browse, then apply. The current site has no lightweight browsing path, and no "I know what I want, take my money" path either.

The fix
Three above-the-fold CTAs per program: Get Info · See Cost · Apply Now.
How the site learns who's who

The redesigned site recognizes each of the four personas before they've typed a single character.

Persona classification isn't a survey. It's a quiet inference made from the signals every visitor already sends: the referral they came from, the page they landed on, the time of day, the device, the scroll depth, and the questions they ask the concierge. The site pairs those signals with a small, transparent model that assigns a best-guess persona and tailors what the next screen emphasizes. Nothing personally identifying is stored without consent; the classification travels with the lead into Slate so advisors know which persona to treat them as.

01 · The signals
What the first few seconds tell us.

By the time a visitor has been on holyfamily.edu for thirty seconds, the site has a dozen signals that line up cleanly with persona. None of them are secret, invasive, or dependent on third-party cookies. They are signals the site already collects and then throws away.

  • Referral & UTM
    "RN-to-BSN Philadelphia" paid search → almost certainly Maria. A LinkedIn ad → Elena. A TikTok referral → Jasmine.
  • Hour of day & device
    Mobile after 8 pm skews adult learner. Laptop at noon skews graduate. Saturday morning skews first-year with a parent.
  • Landing page & scroll
    Transfer-credit page, rapid scroll, direct jump to tuition → Marcus. Program browsing + photo gallery → Jasmine.
  • First concierge question
    "Do you accept CCP credits?" classifies in one turn. "How much is the BSN?" in one turn. The site asks. It doesn't guess forever.
02 · What the site does with it
The inference changes the next screen, not just a dashboard.

Classification is only useful if it shapes what the visitor sees next. The redesigned site personalizes four specific moments (the hero card, the CTA order, the concierge's opening line, and the RFI form's default fields) based on the current best-guess persona. If the guess is wrong, the visitor corrects it in a single tap; the site re-ranks and learns.

What this unlocks for enrollment

Persona recognition is not a UX flourish. It's how the site grows the funnel.

Three measurable outcomes flow from treating the four personas as first-class citizens of the site. None of them require a bigger marketing budget. All three compound month over month as the model learns from real HFU traffic rather than our composites.

01 · Top of funnel
+30–50%
More qualified inquiries from the same traffic.

When the first screen answers the question the persona came with, form completion rises sharply. Industry benchmark for persona-matched landing pages is a 30–50% lift in conversion on the same ad spend. HFU's existing paid search budget suddenly produces meaningfully more leads without a dollar of extra media.

02 · Middle of funnel
−3 days
Faster first contact with the right advisor.

Every RFI arrives in Slate pre-tagged by persona, routed to the correct advisor queue, and scored by engagement depth. Jen calls Maria the morning after instead of three days later. The RFI-to-call gap, currently ~72 hours at peer schools, collapses to a single business day.

03 · Bottom of funnel
2.3×
The compounding Carnegie pattern.

James Vineburgh's earlier concierge build at Carnegie has produced 2,300+ leads since 2025 with no active campaigning. Each conversation trains the classifier, which improves the next conversation. Holy Family's 24-month trajectory on the same model: persona-tagged inquiries compound into measurably better yield and a smaller cost per enrolled student.
